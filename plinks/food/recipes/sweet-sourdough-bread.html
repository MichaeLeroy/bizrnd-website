What's Cooking

I've begun to experiment with sourdough baking and have discovered that it's possible to make a very nice, slightly sweet, cinnamon raisin bread starting with sourdough.

Sources

I can't cite any particular sources for this recipe. When I got my sourdough established and began to look for bread recipes, I found the information available less than completely helpful. Most of the recipes that I found called for conventional yeast as well as the sourdough. I guess that the idea with these sorts hybrid recipes is that the convention yeast provides a quick easy rise, while the sourdough is somewhat relegated to a flavoring. I did find a handful of purely sourdough recipes, but comparing these did not provide me with a clear and consistent picture of how to proceed. So I just started cooking and accepted the risk that some of my results might become chicken feed rather than table fare.

Tools and Utensils

No exotic tools are needed for this recipe. A stand mixer with a dough hook makes the kneading easy, but one can always knead by hand if a mixer is unavailable.

  • A One Quart, Wide Mouth, Mason Jar
  • Stand Mixer with Dough Hook
  • Dry Measuring Cups and Spoons
  • Liquid Measuring Cup
  • A Medium Sized Bowl
  • A Clean Towel
  • A Loaf Pan
  • Oven
  • Cooling Racks

Ingredients

Sourdough Prep

¾ to 1 Cup Whole Wheat Bread Flour

≈1 Cup Chlorine Free Water

Use bottled water, or allow tap water to stand for a day prior to use.

Bread Ingredients

Prepared Sourdough

¼ Cup Milk

¼ Cup Water

¼ Cup Sugar

⅓ Cup of Raisins

1 Teaspoon Cinnamon

A Pinch of Cloves

A Pinch of Mace

A Pinch of Cardamon

2 to 3 Cups All Purpose Flour

Sourdough Preparations

Grow a batch of sourdough from your starter a day prior to baking, feeding it about when you feed yourself. Let the sourdough grow in a warm dark place between these feedings. A 1 quart wide mouth mason jar is a handy container for process.

  • at breakfast, in a 1 quart jar combine 1 tablespoon each of sourdough starter and whole wheat flour with a scant tablespoon of water
  • at lunch, stir in ¼ cup whole wheat flour and a scant ¼ cup of water
  • at supper, stir in ½ cup whole wheat flour and a scant ½ cup of water
  • after growing overnight the culture should be ready for your bread in the morning

Mixing and Kneading

Combine all but the last of the Bread Ingredients (the all purpose flour) in the stand mixer's bowl. Turn the mixer on to a low speed and gradually spoon in the all purpose flour as the dough is kneaded. It may be helpful at times, particularly early on, to pause the mixing and scrape the ingredients into the center of the bowl. As sufficient flour is added, the dough will begin to pull away from the sides of the bowl and to drop off of the mixer's dough hook.

Rising and Forming the Loaf

Once kneading is completed, the dough should be a firm, slightly tacky mass. Flour your hands and gently form the dough into a ball. Put the dough into a medium sized bowl containing enough vegetable oil to coat the dough and roll the dough around to coat it.

Cover the bowl with the clean towel and place it in a warm, dark and quiet place for rising. Sourdoughs rise more slowly than breads made with commercial bread yeast, so it may take 3 to 4 hours for the dough to double in size. If you prepared the dough just after breakfast, it may have finished this first rise by about lunch.

Punch down the dough and transfer it to an oiled loaf pan. Cover the pan with the towel and let the dough rise a second time, so that it again doubles, probably by about supper time.

Baking the Bread

Bake the loaf in a 350℉ oven for 25 to 30 minutes, until the top crust browns slightly. Some have suggested spritzing water into the oven every so often to provide a high humidity environment for the bread. I've not tried this recipe enough times to discern whether this touch has any effect.

Pop the bread out onto a cooling rack to let it cool a bit before enjoying your creation.
